A real brief
from the desert.

A module built around a live project in Egypt’s New Valley Governorate — a region that covers 42% of the country’s land mass and holds most of the Western Desert and its oases. The Governor called for proposals to house a growing population in a sustainable, resource-efficient way. / THE MODULE

Housing the oasis, resource by resource.

Human Settlements in Developing Countries frames the studio around a genuine commission rather than a hypothetical site. The New Valley Governorate — El Wadi El Gadid — is Egypt’s largest governorate by area and one of its most sparsely populated, defined by the Western Desert and a scattered chain of oases. Growing that population sustainably means confronting scarce water, extreme climate and the cost of imported materials head-on.

Working with Dr Nermine AbdelGelil, students carried the brief from site and climatic analysis through to resolved urban proposals for housing extensions at El Kharga City. Each scheme was built on a Compressed Earth Block (CEB) eco-house designed by Dr AbdelGelil — a low-carbon, locally-sourced prototype — scaled up into a sustainable residential quarter with its own energy, grey-water and public-space strategies. The finished proposals were presented in person to the Governor of Al-Wady Al-Gadid. The work shown below is the students’ own.

The semester closed with a formal review in front of Governor General Mohamed Al-Zamlout and his team. Students walked the officials through their site analysis, urban strategy and eco-house prototype board by board — making the case for how a desert settlement could grow without exhausting its water, energy or land.
