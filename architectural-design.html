/ OVERVIEW

Two briefs, two ways of imagining.

These two studios sit either side of 2022. In the Fall Design VI studio I ran an experimental format: students used AI image programs during the conceptual phase, writing narratives about place and use and letting the generated imagery push their first ideas further than a blank page allows. Those concepts were then developed into a resolved mega-structure on the Red Sea coast.

The Spring Design IV studio worked the opposite way — a tightly argued institutional brief, a UNESCO headquarters dedicated to protecting underwater cultural heritage, carried from position to plan and section. Both are presented below in the students’ own boards.

ADesign VI · Fall 2022 · Galala Twin Towers

Studio — 34 students, AI-assisted concept phase

Set on the hilltops of the AlGalala plateau, some 850 metres above the Red Sea, the studio brief combined three programmes — live, work and play — into a single mega-structure aimed at remote workers who want to live in the climate while they work. Students opened the design with AI image generation, translating written narratives into original imagery that seeded each concept.

The exemplar scheme, the Galala Twin Towers, pairs two 300-metre towers on a shared podium of shops and entertainment: one tower given to administrative offices, the other to hotel apartments and residential units, together intended as an iconic landmark over the surrounding landscape.

BDesign IV · Spring 2022 · UNESCO Underwater Heritage HQ

Student project — Mariam Tamer

The Design IV brief argued for a dedicated UNESCO headquarters for the protection of underwater cultural heritage — the shipwrecks, submerged sites and artefacts that record our shared history and are steadily lost to looting, pollution and neglect. The building is proposed as a hub for the research, education and advocacy that such protection needs, and as a way of raising public awareness of the sites themselves.

The scheme below carries that position into architecture, reaching from the shoreline toward the water it is charged with protecting.
